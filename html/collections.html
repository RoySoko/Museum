Hand-Carved Wooden Dog Whistle

Material: Western red cedar body, twisted cedar bark cord, faint traces of spruce resin that still hold a ghost of a forest scent if warmed in the hand.

Size/Weight/Shape: 9.1 cm × 1.6 cm; ~14 g; slender, tapered tube with a whisper-thin mouth opening.

Estimated Age: Precise dating is still under review; the piece is currently undergoing high-resolution stratigraphic scanning and isotope analysis by a joint team of preservation scientists and chronometry specialists to narrow its origin down to the exact year—and possibly even the month—of creation. Estimated Completion Date for Analysis: April 17, 2026.

Preservation State: Complete; cedar shows faint surface checking; cord frayed at the knot as though repeatedly untied and retied in haste.

Location Found: Double Lakes bluff, Site D-2, 32–36 cm depth, silty loam dusted with reed fragments.

Use and Function: Acoustic signal device.

Likely Purpose: To send precise, far-carrying commands to working dogs navigating open water or weaving through dense reed beds — the sort of whistle a handler could lift without breaking stride.

Evidence of Use: Smooth lip polish on the mouth end; two shallow tooth nicks just below the notch — telltale signs of an eager retriever trying to “help” — and a burnished suspension groove worn glossy from years of swinging at a handler’s side.

Manufacture Clues: Central channel hand-bored; knife-cut notch clean but slightly asymmetrical; lathe-smoothed exterior faintly ridged where the grain resisted.

Found With: Leather strap fragment, copper button stamped with a worn hunting motif, seed husks likely from dockside grain sacks.

Burial or Habitat Context: Collapsed bank layer mixed with domestic debris — possibly from a shoreline homestead.

Symbolism: While undecorated, the whistle’s plain form suggests it was meant for work, not show — yet to those who know, the first trill after a long day’s hunt can carry as much emotion as any spoken word.

Comparison: Similar in form to rural handmade whistles documented in Pacific Northwest hunting kits, though the tooth marks make this one unmistakably personal.

Artifact ID
ARC-D2-WHI-001
Cultural Historian
Professor Saadhan M. Anandanai
Title
Hand-Carved Wooden Dog Whistle
Material
Western red cedar body; twisted cedar-bark cord; faint traces of spruce resin.

What the Experts Believe

Material choice. Western red cedar was a practical local wood: light, easy to carve, strong for its weight, and naturally rot- and insect-resistant because of its oils. A twisted cedar-bark cord is common field gear—flexible, quick to knot, and durable when wet. Trace spruce resin on the surface likely acted as a simple sealant or pitch stiffener at the mouth notch; it also helps shed water.

Why a whistle at all. Working handlers need to direct dogs across distance, wind, and reeds where hand signals and speech fail. A whistle carries farther than a voice, is less tiring, and gives repeatable cues that any trained dog can learn. It also lets a handler keep both hands free for oars, rope, or game.

How this one was used. Wear patterns are clear: smooth polish at the lip from frequent playing; a glossy suspension groove from long belt or neck carry; and two shallow tooth marks just below the notch from brief canine contact during retrieval or excitement. The tapered tube and hand-bored airway produce a bright, penetrating tone useful over water. The slightly off-center, knife-cut notch favors fast response over decoration—consistent with field use where seconds matter.

Benefits in the field. Standardized sound codes reduce errors (e.g., “turn,” “hold,” “come”). Dogs hear high pitches well; a small whistle produces those pitches with little breath. The object is cheap to make, easy to repair, and quiet to carry.

Design and known variants. This example is a simple notched tube: tapered cedar body, single airway, single tone. Regional comparanda include:
• wood or bone tube whistles (single tone; closest match to this piece);
• “pea” whistles (wood or metal with a small ball that creates a trill; later/modern);
• dual-chamber whistles (two tones for more complex codes; usually later and often metal or plastic).
Compared with those, this whistle’s minimalist design is typical of earlier, handmade working gear—durable, easy to keep dry, and loud enough for shoreline work.

Context. Associated finds—a leather strap fragment, a copper hunting-motif button, and grain seed husks—fit a provisioning/hunting setting near water. The find layer (collapsed bank with domestic debris) supports ordinary loss rather than ceremonial discard.

Comparative Note

Dimensions, taper, and notch geometry match handmade Pacific Northwest hunting whistles. This specimen differs by its combined evidence set: (1) paired dental impressions below the notch, (2) deep suspension-groove burnish, and (3) surviving resin traces. Few reference pieces preserve all three, indicating a long, practical service life with a specific handler–dog team rather than generic or unused equipment.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Pitch, signal vocabulary, and training routines cannot be reconstructed directly from the object. Non-canine signaling (e.g., to people) is possible but less supported by the context. Absolute dating and cultural assignment remain provisional pending the ongoing stratigraphic and isotope analyses (report expected April 17, 2026). Interpretations may change with new data.

ARC-C1-DOL-001
Cultural Historian
Professor Saadhan M. Anandanai
Title
Wirehaired Pointing Griffon Doll
Material
Hand-tanned deerskin panels stitched with sinew; stuffing of dried moss and shed canine undercoat; eyes of polished river stones flecked with mica; detailing in natural ochre and charcoal.

What the Experts Believe

Material choice. Deerskin was locally obtainable, soft to handle, and durable at seams; hand-tanning leaves residual oils that resist cracking. Sinew provides high tensile strength when dry and can be re-tightened with moisture—ideal for small stitches. Moss is light, cleanable, and readily packed; adding shed dog undercoat increases loft and retains a familiar household scent. River stones are smooth, non-splintering eye elements. Ochre/charcoal pigments were common, safe colorants for simple patterning.

Why a doll at all. In working households, a dog was both tool and companion. A small zoomorphic doll allows a child to model handling routines (stay, settle, heel), provides comfort during hunts or long work days, and represents family identity—“our dog”—without risk or cost.

How this one was used. Wear is concentrated at the muzzle and flanks (rubbing and hugging). One ear is worn to its stitching; a side seam has opened to reveal moss, consistent with extended handling rather than display. A faint smoke odor indicates long residence near a hearth. The object was deliberately hidden 4 cm below an attic floor gap, a common child cache or safekeeping tactic.

Benefits in daily life. The doll is light, portable, and quiet; it survives falls and rough play better than clay figures. The undercoat likely preserved a recognizable scent, reinforcing attachment and making the object calming during separation from the working dog.

Design and known variants. Construction is a panel-stitched leather form with seated posture and slight head tilt. Regional comparanda include:
• Rag or textile dolls (cloth panels, plant-fiber stuffing);
• Cornhusk figures (braided husks, plant-dye markings);
• Leather-stuffed animals (deerskin or elk hide, moss/hair fill).
This specimen’s Griffon-specific detailing (short pigment strokes suggesting wiry coat; river-stone eyes with mica glint) is atypical of generic farm toys and implies reference to a particular household dog.

Context. Associated items in the cavity—two smooth game stones, a splintered clay bead, a short woven-grass cord, and graphite doodles of a person walking a dog—fit a child activity zone rather than storage of valuables. The dry, dust-sealed attic insulated the object from insects and temperature swings, explaining the good pigment survival.

Comparative Note

Parallels exist among early settler zoomorphic toys, but this example differs on three counts: (1) species-specific styling consistent with a Wirehaired Pointing Griffon, (2) scent-linked stuffing using shed canine undercoat, and (3) deliberate concealment context in a child’s cache. Together these point to a personalized object tied to a named working dog rather than a generic trade toy.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Owner age, gender, and exact making circumstances are unknown. The undercoat may derive from grooming waste rather than ritual inclusion. While function as a child’s toy is well supported by wear and context, secondary roles (talisman, keepsake after an animal’s death) cannot be confirmed. Absolute dating remains under review (report expected Sep 3, 2026); all cultural attributions are provisional pending laboratory results.

Title
Huckleberry-Dyed Woolen Sock of Joy
Material
Hand-spun wool dyed with crushed huckleberry skins and rainwater; toe and heel reinforced with nettle-fiber thread.

What the Experts Believe

Material choice. Hand-spun wool was the standard cold-weather textile: warm, elastic, and repairable. Huckleberry skins provide an accessible plant dye; uneven, streaked uptake is typical of small-batch vats without metallic mordants. Nettle fiber is strong and abrasion-resistant, making it a sensible reinforcement for heel and toe—known stress points.

Primary function. Everyday clothing. The intact heel and packed wool along the instep indicate long wear inside a shoe or boot. Dye mottling from repeated wet-dry cycles is consistent with winter use.

Secondary use-life. Damage signatures shift later: a stretched cuff from tugging, wide frays at the toe, and punctures arranged in semicircles. Pattern and spacing fit canine dentition better than rodent gnaw. Faint muddy paw prints on the instep confirm handling by a dog, likely during play or retrieval.

Benefits in daily life. Wool insulates when damp; a single sock is simple to mend; and natural dyes allow home production. As a toy, a worn sock carries household scent, explaining its attraction to a working or young dog and the “tug” wear observed.

Design and known variants. Even-gauge knitting with reinforced heel/toe is typical of settler households. Regional comparanda include:
• plain-undyed wool socks (coarser gauge; no reinforcement);
• plant-dyed socks using berry, bark, or lichen baths;
• patterned socks (two-color motifs; rarer in frontier contexts).
This specimen is distinguished by the dye streaking characteristic of small vessels and by clear canine interaction marks, which are uncommon in curated textile finds.

Context. Recovered from a hearthside debris layer at Cabin Site 2 (12–18 cm depth) within a domestic floor collapse with charred timbers—conditions that produced cool, low-light preservation. Associated finds (splintered stick, small river stone, broken clay cup handle) match household refuse rather than ritual deposition.

Comparative Note

Fiber type and knit gauge match other early-settler socks from regional collections. Two features set this object apart: (1) diagnostic canine tooth punctures paired with a stretched cuff indicative of tugging, and (2) huckleberry dye variegation showing small-batch processing. Together they document a transition from garment to informal canine play object—rarely recorded so clearly in textiles.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

While the puncture pattern is consistent with a dog, limited lab work leaves a small possibility of multi-agent damage. Dye identification is based on macroscopic color and contextual notes; chromatographic confirmation is pending. Absolute dating remains under review (report expected Feb 11, 2027). Interpretations may be revised as analysis proceeds.

Cultural Historian
Professor Saadhan M. Anandanai
Title
Cluster of Wirehaired Pointing Griffon Puppy Teeth
Material
Deciduous canine and incisor teeth (hydroxyapatite-rich enamel over dentine with residual root cementum) in fine loam with spruce-needle traces.

What the Experts Believe

Material and biology. These are naturally shed deciduous teeth from a young dog. Puppy enamel is hard and glossy; thin cementum at the root end may remain when a tooth falls out. The presence of crowns with faint root remnants fits the normal transition to adult dentition.

Why these appear on sites. Puppies shed teeth between ~3–6 months of age. Teeth are small, easily lost during play, chewing, or mouthing. In kennel or bedding areas, they accumulate where animals rest.

Use/function. Not a tool or ornament—these are biological remains of routine tooth replacement.

Wear and taphonomy. Microscopic linear striations on occlusal/labial surfaces match chewing on fibrous plants or small bones. Low micro-pitting and clean cementum margins indicate gentle, in-place deposition rather than trampling.

Deposition and context. The cluster was recovered at Site K-3 on the edge of a sunken kennel foundation, 8–12 cm deep in compact bedding soil. Associated finds—matted undercoat fur, a short stick worn smooth at both ends, and a fragment of woven reed mat—are typical of a bedding/play zone. Low moisture, shelter, and limited freeze–thaw cycles explain the exceptional preservation.

Interpretive value. A tight scatter (~6.2 cm) suggests the teeth were shed near a favored resting spot rather than distributed by cleaning or runoff. Together with undercoat and chew-stick evidence, the cluster documents a brief developmental window in a working dog household.

Comparative Note

Comparable modern and archaeological finds often yield isolated deciduous teeth. In contrast, this intact in-situ cluster with bedding indicators (fur clump, smoothed chew stick, reed-mat fragment) is uncommon. The preservation state (glossy enamel, minimal micro-pitting) and compact scatter strengthen the interpretation of a kennel bedding context rather than general yard or midden deposition.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Individual identity and exact litter size cannot be determined from the cluster alone; multiple pups may be represented. Micro-striations are consistent with chewing but are not species-exclusive to plant or bone substrates. Absolute dating remains under review (report expected Nov 8, 2026) based on sediment micro-layering and trace-element analysis. Conclusions may be revised with new data (e.g., collagen testing, sediment micromorphology).

Cultural Historian
Professor Saadhan M. Anandanai
Title
Partial Human Hand and Distal Radius
Material
Human skeletal elements (partial metacarpals, proximal phalanges, distal radius) in cortical bone with preserved microstructure; trace mineral staining from surrounding soil.

What the Experts Believe

Anatomy and properties. These are adult human hand bones and the distal end of the radius. Cortical bone (the dense outer layer) resists bending and preserves surface micro-wear. Mineral staining and fine soil concretions are typical for compact silty clay.

Use-related signals. Along the radial sides of the metacarpals, shallow, linear micropolish forms a narrow track—consistent with repeated low-friction contact by skin or hair. On the distal radius, there is mild osteophytic lipping and subtle remodeling at tendon attachment points. The pattern indicates long-term, low-load, repetitive flexion–extension rather than heavy or asymmetric labor. Combined, these features fit a habitual, forward, low-elevation hand motion (e.g., stroking at knee height) more than tool grasping or forceful grip.

Context and deposition. The elements were recovered beneath the east threshold stone of Cabin Site 7 at 44–52 cm depth in compact silty clay—stable temperature and humidity; minimal rodent disturbance. Threshold placement is a purposeful, protected location that often marks a liminal boundary (entry/exit) in domestic spaces. Associated finds (wool cuff fragment, small bronze button, leather sliver with buckle remnant) indicate ordinary clothing rather than funerary goods.

Why this matters. Occupational stress markers (weaving, archery, smithing) usually produce joint asymmetries and enthesophytes with load signatures different from those observed here. The polish track and mild, evenly distributed changes align with routine, gentle contact repeated over years. In a household with working dogs, a stroking/patting behavior is a plausible driver for the specific polish location and motion range, though it cannot be proven as the sole cause.

Preservation notes. No burning; no major fragmentation. Fine cracking along phalangeal shafts is typical age-related or burial microfracture. Surface detail is adequate for microscopy and future histology if needed.

Comparative Note

Reference collections show task-specific patterns: weavers often present metacarpophalangeal changes with different polish orientation; archers display unilateral remodeling at the forearm consistent with draw/brace asymmetry; smiths show localized enthesopathies at power-grip insertions. The bilateral-neutral, low-load signal and narrow radial-side polish track on metacarpals observed here are uncommon in those categories. A small number of domestic-context cases with “contact polish” from frequent animal handling provide the closest analogs, though published examples remain rare.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Individual identity, sex, and handedness are not established. The polish track indicates repeated contact but is not activity-exclusive; textiles, fur, or repetitive household tasks could contribute. Absolute dating is under review (combined osteometrics and U–Th approach; report expected Jun 14, 2027). Interpretations remain provisional pending lab results and any additional context from the cabin stratigraphy.

Title

What the Experts Believe

Anatomy and properties. Single right (likely) femur, length 19.0 cm, slight anteroposterior curvature, ~110 g. The cortical shell is intact with minor surface weathering; no cut marks, percussion scars, or burning observed. Gnawing on the distal epiphysis consists of small, shallow scoops consistent with light scavenger activity. Faint root etching along the midshaft indicates contact with growing vegetation during burial.

Function/status. Anatomical remain; no evidence of modification for tool use or ornament. Morphometrics place the element within the range of a medium–large domestic dog (≈25–30 kg); proportions are compatible with Wirehaired Pointing Griffon reference sets.

Context and deposition. Recovered from a riverbank erosion face (Site E-3), nestled in a shallow silt-and-root alcove beneath overhanging alder. Sediment is a mixed fluvial/colluvial horizon over compact sand—an environment where bones can be reworked by seasonal bank slumps. Orientation (facing downstream) and shelter under roots could result from either deliberate placement or low-energy redeposition.

Associated finds. A smooth river stone with a single shallow groove (possible handstone wear), a plaited leather cord fragment with frayed end, and a bronze bead comparable to fittings seen on historical canine collars from the Royce era. Together these reinforce a domestic-dog context but stop short of proving individual identity.

Interpretive value. The specimen documents the presence of a sizeable working dog in the riverine household economy. Lack of butchery marks argues against kitchen waste; light gnawing and root etching chart a short sequence of exposure, minor scavenger attention, then quiet burial in a protected niche.

Comparative Note

Regional collections show similar femoral lengths and shaft curvatures in 25–30 kg sporting and working breeds. Unlike common midden finds, this piece lacks cut/burn signals and appears with collar-adjacent material (bronze bead, cord). That association is unusual for random shoreline redeposition and is more often seen in curated or carefully placed animal remains, though the fluvial setting prevents a firm conclusion.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Attribution to a specific dog or lineage (e.g., “Royce”) is not supportable without broader skeletal association, DNA, or diagnostic artifacts directly fixed to the bone. Fluvial processes may rework both bone and associated items. Absolute dating is under review via stratigraphic scanning and isotope analysis (report expected Nov 14, 2029). Morphometric matches are probabilistic, not definitive; conclusions remain provisional pending laboratory results.
